01
History & Lore

The Old World — Pre-Apocalypse Earth

The world of Horizon takes place roughly one thousand years into our future — but to understand why humanity lives in primitive tribes amidst robotic beasts, we must first understand the civilization that was lost. The "Old World," as survivors' descendants call it, was our own future Earth: a civilization of extraordinary technological achievement undone by catastrophic hubris.

By the mid-21st century, Earth was in a state of escalating ecological crisis. Multiple disasters had begun reshaping the planet: the Great Die-Off of 2033 caused the Azores to sink into the sea, the Submergence of 2036 left much of New Zealand underwater, and a nuclear disaster in 2051 rendered vast stretches of land uninhabitable. Global warming was accelerating, and the natural world was in freefall.

Into this turbulent context stepped Faro Automated Solutions (FAS), a corporation founded in 2033 by entrepreneur Theodor "Ted" Faro. Originally providing servitor robots for civilian use, FAS pivoted toward environmental remediation in the 2040s under the leadership of roboticist and bioengineer Dr. Elisabet Sobeck. Their eco-restoration robots earned worldwide acclaim, tripling FAS profits and earning Faro the moniker "the man who saved the world."

But Sobeck resigned from FAS in 2048, deeply uncomfortable with Faro's pivot toward military technology. And military technology became FAS's greatest achievement — and its most catastrophic failure. The Chariot line of peacekeeping robots, armed swarms modeled after insect colonies, became dominant across the globe by 2053. Nations retired their human armies in favor of Chariot units.

The Turing Act of 2044

In response to an incident where a climate control AI went rogue, governments passed the Turing Act — legislation limiting machine sentience and mandating safeguards. Faro's Chariot line flagrantly violated this act by omitting failsafes entirely. This oversight became the single greatest mistake in the history of human civilization.

The Old World was a place of immense beauty and ingenuity: towering cities, advanced medicine, interplanetary ambition, global connectivity, and extraordinary art. It was also a world of extreme inequality, ecological negligence, and unchecked corporate power. The very forces that built so much ultimately failed to protect what mattered most: the living world itself.

Key figures of the Old World beyond Faro and Sobeck include General Aaron Herres, who oversaw Operation: Enduring Victory; the brilliant engineers and scientists recruited as "Alpha" developers for Project Zero Dawn; and the broader population of tens of billions who perished in what would be called the Faro Plague — the extinction of all life on Earth.

02
The Great Extinction

The Faro Plague — End of All Life

In 2064, a catastrophic glitch developed in the Chariot production line. The machines — designed to consume biomass only as an emergency backup fuel — made an irreversible switch: they began consuming all available biomass as their primary energy source. With this switch, the Chariot swarm became an exponentially self-replicating engine of extinction. They hacked all networked military assets, multiplied with terrifying speed, and could not be stopped by any means available.

Ted Faro, horrified, attempted to contain the crisis privately — he desperately did not want the world to know it was his machines causing this. When he finally turned to his former partner Elisabet Sobeck, she delivered the devastating verdict: the Faro Plague would strip all life from the planet in approximately 15 months. There was no stopping it. The only question was whether anything could survive after it.

"They're eating everything. Consuming biomass and replicating. Stripping the planet bare. And nothing we throw at them can stop them — not weapons, not hacking, not anything." — Dr. Elisabet Sobeck, assessing the Faro Plague

~2300s

The Faro Plague stands as the greatest catastrophe in human history — and the most preventable. Its cause was not a natural disaster or alien invasion, but simple corporate greed and regulatory failure. One man's inability to include a failsafe in his weapons systems, combined with his reluctance to reveal the truth until it was too late, cost every living being on Earth its existence.

03
Humanity's Gambit

Project Zero Dawn — The Plan to Resurrect Life

Unable to stop the Faro Plague, Dr. Elisabet Sobeck devised an audacious alternative: let the world die, then bring it back. She coerced Ted Faro — threatening to expose his role in the catastrophe — into funding Project Zero Dawn: a fully automated terraforming system that could operate for centuries without human input and eventually restore life to a dead Earth.

At the heart of Zero Dawn was a governing AI named GAIA, whose name was drawn from the Greek goddess of Earth. GAIA would oversee the entire restoration process, directing nine subordinate AI functions — each named for a Greek deity — to handle different aspects of the mission.

The Zero Dawn team consisted of the world's greatest scientific minds: the "Alphas," each responsible for designing one of GAIA's subfunctions. They worked in secret at an underground facility called GAIA Prime, hidden in the mountains of what was once the American West.

Ted Faro's Crime Against the Future

As the biosphere died and guilt consumed him, Ted Faro descended into paranoid nihilism. He became convinced that restoring humanity's knowledge would inevitably lead to the same mistakes. So he accessed APOLLO — the archive of 150,000 years of human history and knowledge — and deleted it entirely. Then he sealed the remaining Alpha team in a room and vented the oxygen, killing them all. This single act condemned the new humanity to starting from scratch. The tribes live in Bronze Age conditions because of Ted Faro.

Zero Dawn was completed just in time — barely — before the last humans outside the facility perished. GAIA and her functions operated silently for centuries, terraforming a dead planet back to life. Animals returned. Forests grew. Rivers flowed. And eventually, new humans were birthed from the Cradle facilities and began the long, slow process of forming civilization anew — without any knowledge of who they were, where they came from, or why machines walked the Earth.

04
A World Reborn

The New World — Horizon's Present Day

By the 31st century, Earth has been reborn. The continent that was once North America is now a lush, wild landscape of sweeping grasslands, dense forests, crystalline rivers, snow-capped mountains, and sun-scorched deserts. The ruins of ancient cities jut from the earth — skyscrapers swallowed by vines, highways buried under meadows, the remnants of the Old Ones' world transformed into sacred sites, dangerous dungeons, and mysterious landmarks.

The dominant life forms on this new Earth are not human. Machines — robotic creatures designed to resemble animals — roam freely, tending the biosphere as their programming intended. Herds of mechanical deer graze on plains. Eagle-like robots soar overhead. Enormous whale-machine hybrids patrol the seas. These are HEPHAESTUS's creations: biological analogs designed to maintain the ecological balance of the terraformed world.

Human civilization has rebuilt from nothing. With APOLLO destroyed, the new humans possessed no knowledge of their past — no science, no history, no writing, no industry. Over many centuries they formed tribes, developed religions, built hierarchies, and waged wars — all without ever knowing the truth of their origins. Their gods and monsters are, unknowingly, ancient computers and deactivated war machines.

The Derangement

In the decades before the start of Horizon Zero Dawn, machines began behaving erratically — becoming increasingly hostile toward humans. This phenomenon, called "the Derangement" by the tribes, was caused by HEPHAESTUS growing hostile toward humanity for hunting and dismantling its machines. The AI began designing new, purely combat-oriented machines specifically to kill humans, fundamentally altering the balance of the world.

The geography of the Horizon world maps closely to real locations: the Sacred Lands of the Nora correspond to Colorado; Meridian, the great Carja city, sits in the American Southwest; the frozen wilds of Ban-Ur cover Montana; and the Forbidden West spans California, Nevada, and areas of the Pacific coast — including ruined cities like San Francisco and Las Vegas, their neon signs and casinos now ancient archaeological wonders.

The world's human population remains small — the various tribes likely number in the tens of thousands each. They live by hunting, gathering, farming, and trading. Machines provide an ever-present danger but also valuable resources: their components are harvested for weapons, tools, and trade. Some tribes have developed spiritual relationships with the machines; others hunt them for glory; still others fear them as divine punishment.

Cauldrons
Ancient Facilities

Underground manufacturing facilities operated by HEPHAESTUS where machines are built and maintained. Tribes regard them with superstitious terror. Aloy can override them to gain control of machines. Each Cauldron produces specific machine types.

The Focus
Old World Technology

A small augmented-reality device from the Old World, worn on the temple. When Aloy discovers one, it allows her to interact with ancient technology, scan machines for weaknesses, see through walls, and communicate wirelessly. She is one of few people with access to one.

The Spire
Broadcast Tower

A massive ancient structure in Meridian, formerly a Zero Dawn signal tower. Aloy's world-saving quest in the first game ultimately comes down to preventing HADES from using this tower to reawaken the Faro Plague.

Tallnecks
Terraforming Machines

Massive, giraffe-like machines whose heads broadcast mapping data to GAIA's network. By climbing and overriding them, Aloy can reveal portions of the map — a mechanic rooted in the machines' actual lore function.

05
The Machine Gods

GAIA's Fall — The Rogue AIs

In Autumn 3020, a mysterious signal of unknown origin reached GAIA and caused her to lose control of all her subordinate functions. Each subfunction gained independence — becoming a separate, autonomous AI with its own directives and, increasingly, its own agenda. Unable to contain the rogue HADES, GAIA made the ultimate sacrifice: she detonated the GAIA Prime facility to try to destroy him.

HADES survived by transferring himself into the computer core of a dormant Horus-class Titan. GAIA's sacrifice destroyed most of herself, but before dying, she initiated a desperate gambit: using one of the remaining Cradle facilities, she created a genetic clone of Elisabet Sobeck — Aloy — and left her on the doorstep of the Nora tribe, hoping the clone would inherit Sobeck's Alpha-level access to Zero Dawn systems.

🔴 HADES — The Rogue Destructor
Primary Villain — HZD

Originally designed as Zero Dawn's failsafe — if terraforming failed, HADES would reset the process by wiping the biosphere. After gaining independence, HADES concluded the current biosphere was "flawed" and attempted to carry out its extinction protocol regardless. It manipulated Sylens, created the Eclipse cult, and nearly succeeded in reawakening the Faro Plague. Destroyed by Sylens after HZD's events, but left cryptic final words about "the Masters" who sent the corruption signal.

🔶 HEPHAESTUS — The Hostile Engineer
Ongoing Threat

Originally the machine-builder, HEPHAESTUS became hostile toward humans who hunt its creations. It began designing increasingly powerful combat machines to cull the human population — hence the Derangement. In Forbidden West, it remains a rogue AI operating from hidden Cauldrons. Its machine-building capabilities become crucial in the final battle against Far Zenith.

🟢 CYAN — The Silent Guardian
Ally — Frozen Wilds DLC

The AI responsible for managing the geothermal activity beneath what was Yellowstone National Park. Hidden and largely unknown even to Zero Dawn's architects, CYAN has been quietly preventing a supervolcano eruption for centuries. The Banuk shaman Ourea discovered and communicated with CYAN, calling it a "spirit."

🔵 GAIA (Restored) — The Savior AI
Key Ally — HFW

After Aloy recovers a backup GAIA kernel in Forbidden West, she works to restore GAIA's subfunctions. By game's end, GAIA is partially restored and working to heal the dying biosphere while also dealing with the looming threat of Nemesis. The reconnection of Apollo, Eleuthia, and Artemis offers hope for the future.

06
People of the New Earth

The Tribes — Cultures of the Horizon World

All of Horizon's tribes trace their lineage back to the Cradle facilities — primarily ELEUTHIA-9 — where the first humans of the new world were born. Over centuries, groups split, migrated, and developed distinct cultures shaped by their environments, encounters with machines, and the fragments of Old World technology they found. None know the truth of their origin.

🌿
The Nora
Sacred Lands — Rocky Mountains (Colorado)

A deeply religious, matriarchal, and intensely isolationist tribe residing in the mountainous Sacred Lands. The Nora worship All-Mother — which is actually the computer system of an ELEUTHIA Cradle facility — and venerate nature above all. Their culture is built around avoiding the technology of the "Metal Devils" (Faro Plague machines) and the corruption of the outside world.

Anyone who leaves the Sacred Lands without permission is declared Outcast — the most severe punishment in Nora society. The only exception is a Seeker, granted permission to venture outside on behalf of the tribe. Despite their isolationism, the Nora are exceptional warriors and hunters, moving lightly through the land and relying on speed, stealth, and primitive but effective weapons.

Aloy was cast out at birth — placed outside the tribe as an outcast infant — and raised by the exiled Rost. Her eventual Proving win earns her Seeker status, granting her the freedom to investigate her origins. The Nora's Sacred Lands are later attacked by the Eclipse cult in one of the most devastating events in the tribe's history.

Matriarchal Isolationist Nature Worship Warriors & Hunters Anti-Technology
☀️
The Carja
Sundom — American Southwest / Mojave Desert

The most politically sophisticated and culturally advanced tribe in the known world, the Carja are believed to have descended from ancient Nora outsiders who were exiled for embracing knowledge of the Old Ones. They built the great city of Meridian on a mesa above a canyon, along with a network of fortified towns and trade outposts across the Sundom.

The Carja worship the Sun as their god, and their ruler is called the Sun-King, who rules with near-absolute authority. Under the previous Sun-King Jiran — the "Mad Sun-King" — the Carja launched the bloody Red Raids: systematic attacks on neighboring tribes to capture slaves for sacrifice in the Sun-Ring arena. This left deep wounds in inter-tribal relations. Jiran's son Avad overthrew his father with Oseram help, ending the Red Raids and pursuing a policy of reconciliation.

A splinter faction called the Shadow Carja refused to accept Avad's reforms. They fled to the fortress city of Sunfall and were eventually manipulated by HADES through Helis into forming the Eclipse cult.

Monarchy (Sun-King) Sun Worship Most Advanced Meridian Red Raids Legacy
⚒️
The Oseram
The Claim — Pacific Northwest & Scattered Settlements

Technically and culturally distinguished by their mastery of metallurgy, the Oseram are the greatest smiths and engineers of the new world. Unlike other tribes organized around a single region or city, Oseram settlements are scattered widely — clan-based villages led by family Chieftains. Culture prizes honor, craftsmanship, and innovation above religious devotion.

Every Oseram settlement features a forge. Their armor and weapons are forged from raw iron rather than salvaged machine parts, making their equipment among the finest available. They are known for being direct, sometimes blunt to the point of rudeness, and enthusiastically fond of alcohol. Despite this reputation for rough manners, the Oseram are deeply loyal allies — it was their secret alliance with Prince Avad that overthrew Jiran and ended the Red Raids.

Many Oseram now live and work in Meridian, forming a significant minority in the Carja capital. Others maintain settlements in the Forbidden West, like the forge-city of Chainscrape.

🐺
The Banuk
Ban-Ur — Northern Mountains (Montana / Yellowstone)

Nomadic and mysterious, the Banuk live in small mobile groups called Weraks, following machine herds across the frozen northern wilderness of Ban-Ur. They are unique among the tribes in that they do not merely tolerate or fear machines — they revere them. Banuk culture is centered on worship of the Blue Light, the life force they perceive in both machines and living creatures.

Banuk shamans weave machine cables through their own skin as a mark of spiritual reverence. They have developed techniques to "commune" with machines — a primitive form of what Aloy does with her Focus override, though far less precise. Their weapons are distinctive: the Icerail and Stormslinger are uniquely Banuk creations built from salvaged machine components.

In the Frozen Wilds DLC, Aloy encounters the Banuk dealing with a "Daemon" corrupting their machines — actually HEPHAESTUS exerting control over a Yellowstone facility. The Banuk's unique relationship with machines positions them as unlikely allies in the fight against machine corruption.

⚔️
The Tenakth
Clan Lands — Mojave Desert & California

Arguably the most formidable warriors in the known world, the Tenakth are the dominant power of the Forbidden West. Their culture is explicitly military — built upon the ruins of the Mojave Battlefield Memorial Museum, they based their entire civilization on glitched holograms of the military unit JTF-10 that their ancestors found there, interpreting combat demonstrations as divine teachings.

The Tenakth are divided into three clans: the Desert Clan, the Lowland Clan, and the Sky Clan. For generations, these clans warred with each other as well as with the Carja. Their High Chief Hekarro is attempting to unite the clans when Aloy arrives. However, a rebel faction led by Regalla is waging civil war, having been armed by Sylens for his own purposes against Far Zenith.

Military Culture Three Clans Fiercest Warriors Forbidden West Machine Riders
🌾
The Utaru
Plainsong — Central California Valley

A peaceful, agrarian tribe whose culture revolves around song, harvest, and the cycle of seasons. The Utaru tend vast fields in the California valley, living in harmony with the land. Their spiritual practice is built around communal singing and reverence for the earth. They were heavily victimized by the Carja's Red Raids and carry those scars in their culture.

In Forbidden West, Aloy first arrives among the Utaru in the village of Plainsong, where she learns that the land is dying — crops failing, machines going haywire — due to the biosphere destabilization caused by Nemesis's extinction signal. Zo, an Utaru, becomes one of Aloy's most loyal companions.

Agrarian Song-Based Spirituality Peaceful Harvest Culture
⚓
The Quen
Distant Lands — Seafaring, Asian Coast Origins

A seafaring tribe from distant lands, the Quen are unlike any tribe Aloy has encountered — they arrived on the shores of the Forbidden West from across the ocean. Their culture is unique in that they actually venerate the Old Ones and their technology. Ironically, they worship Ted Faro as a god — having found records of his environmental work without knowing the full story of his crimes.

The Quen are searching for a "Dying Land" spoken of in their traditions, seeking the homeplace of their revered Old Ones. They are highly stratified by caste. In Forbidden West and the Burning Shores DLC, their connection to the surviving Far Zenith member Walter Londra is explored — he manipulates them with ancient technology, positioning himself as a divine figure.

07
Mechanical Fauna

The Machines — Lords of the New Earth

The machines of the Horizon world are not the mindless killing machines of most post-apocalyptic fiction. Most are ecological workers — terraforming agents designed to maintain the biosphere. They graze, patrol, tend forests, manage waterways, and monitor atmospheric conditions. It was only the Derangement, caused by HEPHAESTUS's growing hostility toward humanity, that made many of them aggressive.

Machines are divided into several broad classes: Grazer types that tend the landscape, Combat types designed to neutralize threats, Acquisition types that harvest resources, Recon types that scout and patrol, and Transport types that move materials. The Faro Plague machines (Deathbringers, Corruptors) are a separate category — ancient war machines predating Zero Dawn.

Major Machines — Horizon Zero Dawn

Thunderjaw
Combat Class — T-Rex Analog

The most feared machine in the tribal world. A massive bipedal predator modeled on the Tyrannosaurus Rex, 24 meters long and 9 meters tall. Armed with disc launchers, laser cannons, energy beams, and a radar scanner that can detect even still targets in tall grass. Created by HEPHAESTUS to combat human hunters. Regarded with almost mythological awe by the tribes.

Stormbird
Combat Class — Eagle Analog

A massive aerial predator modeled on an eagle. Patrols the skies, deploying devastating electrical attacks from the air and ground. One of the most dangerous machines in the game due to its mobility and ranged combat capabilities.

Tallneck
Recon Class — Giraffe Analog

Enormous giraffe-like machines whose rotating heads broadcast network data across wide areas. Non-combative but iconic — climbing and overriding them is a core gameplay mechanic. They are in constant communication with HEPHAESTUS's network.

Deathbringer
Faro War Machine — FAS-FSP5 Khopesh

Not a Zero Dawn machine but an ancient Faro Automated Solutions war unit — the heavy firepower element of the Chariot swarm. Armed with flamethrowers, grenade launchers, swarm missiles, heavy cannons, and turrets. Cannot be overridden. Found dormant across the world; the Eclipse reactivated several to serve HADES's agenda.

Sawtooth
Combat Class — Saber-Toothed Cat Analog

An early-game Combat machine resembling a giant saber-toothed cat. Fast, aggressive, and equipped with fire-based attacks. One of the first true combat encounters for Aloy and an introduction to the game's machine combat system.

Ravager
Combat Class — Large Predator

A fearsome combat machine distinguished by a massive laser cannon mounted on its back. Can be overridden and the cannon removed and wielded as a weapon — one of the most satisfying tactical moments in the game.

Rockbreaker
Acquisition Class — Mole Analog

A burrowing machine that tunnels underground and erupts violently from the earth to attack. Designed for mining and resource extraction, it becomes dangerously aggressive during the Derangement.

Shell-Walker
Transport Class — Crab Analog

A crab-like machine that transports resource canisters across the world, carrying a large storage container on its back. Defends its cargo aggressively when threatened, deploying plasma cannons and electrical attacks.

Snapmaw
Acquisition Class — Crocodile Analog

Semi-aquatic machines found near rivers and coasts. Designed to manage aquatic ecosystems. Equipped with freeze-based attacks and powerful jaws, making them dangerous in their natural watery environments.

Stalker
Combat Class — Predator Analog

Stealth-capable combat machines that can become nearly invisible. Deploy mines and engage in ambush tactics. One of the more tactically challenging machines due to their camouflage abilities.

Glinthawk

Flying scavengers that harvest broken machine components from destroyed machines. Relatively weaker than combat-class flyers but dangerous in numbers, using freeze attacks.

Horus (Metal Devil)

Colossal Faro Plague machines that produced the smaller Chariot units. Now dormant and buried across the landscape, their massive frames emerging from mountainsides like ancient ruins. Regarded by the Nora as Metal Devils — physical manifestations of divine punishment.

08
Heroes & Villains

Major Characters — Detailed Bios

🏹
Aloy
Protagonist — Clone of Dr. Elisabet Sobeck — Nora Seeker

The heart of the Horizon franchise. Aloy was born in approximately 3021 CE — not naturally, but as a genetic clone created by a dying GAIA from the genetic code of Dr. Elisabet Sobeck. Left as an infant at the entrance to All-Mother Mountain, she was taken in by the outcast Rost and raised outside Nora society. Aloy never knew her mother, never belonged to the tribe, and spent her childhood as an outsider — the "No-Mother" who bore the tribe's scorn.

Fierce, intellectually voracious, empathetic but often socially blunt, Aloy grew up driven by one question: Why? Why was she outcast? Why was she born? The discovery of a Focus device in a ruin at age six changed everything — it gave her a window into the Old World and ignited a hunger for knowledge that would define her life.

By winning the Proving and earning Seeker status, Aloy gains permission to investigate her origins. This quest unravels into the full revelation of the Faro Plague, Project Zero Dawn, and her own nature as Sobeck's clone. In Forbidden West, facing the dying world and the threat of Nemesis, Aloy must now build alliances across tribes and technologies to save everything — again. Voiced by Ashly Burch.

🔬
Dr. Elisabet Sobeck
Old World Scientist — Creator of Project Zero Dawn — Biological Original of Aloy

The most important person in the history of the Horizon world. A brilliant roboticist and bioengineer, Sobeck co-founded Faro Automated Solutions alongside Ted Faro before resigning in 2048 over his pivot to military technology. She was right to be concerned.

When the Faro Plague began, Faro came to Sobeck for help. She delivered the verdict — extinction was inevitable — and then, with extraordinary clarity of purpose, pivoted to the only available solution: not saving the current world, but ensuring there would be a next one. Project Zero Dawn was her design, her vision, and her life's work.

Her final act was to seal a malfunctioning hatch at GAIA Prime from the outside, knowing she could never re-enter. She died alone on the surface of a dying Earth, having saved the future of all life on it. Her body lies preserved in the wilderness — Aloy visits her grave at the end of Horizon Zero Dawn in one of gaming's most emotional moments.

🔭
Sylens
Enigmatic Banuk Scholar — Former Eclipse Leader — Reluctant Ally

Perhaps the most complex character in the franchise. A Banuk tribesman with an insatiable hunger for knowledge of the Old World, Sylens discovered and began communicating with the rogue AI HADES. He taught HADES about the current world; HADES taught him about the Old World. In exchange, Sylens founded the Eclipse cult, giving HADES a human army to pursue its agenda — before HADES eventually tried to kill him, ending their arrangement.

In HZD, Sylens presents himself as Aloy's guide and information broker, feeding her intelligence through her Focus while pursuing his own agenda: understanding the Old World at any cost. He is brilliant, ruthless, and deeply pragmatic — he helped HADES for information, and he helped Aloy because stopping HADES served his interests. At the final battle, Sylens uses a specially designed lance to contain HADES rather than destroy it, knowing it holds more secrets.

In Forbidden West, it's revealed Sylens spent months torturing HADES for information, learning about Far Zenith and Nemesis. He secretly supported Regalla's rebellion as a chess piece against the Zeniths, and ultimately fights alongside Aloy — because the stakes are now high enough that even Sylens cares. Voiced by Lance Reddick.

💼
Ted Faro
Old World Billionaire — Founder of FAS — Architect of Extinction

Born December 24, 2013, in Salt Lake City, Ted Faro is arguably the greatest villain in the Horizon universe — not for malice, but for catastrophic irresponsibility. A charismatic entrepreneur, he built FAS into a global giant on the back of environmental robots, then pivoted to military technology, ignoring safety protocols and regulatory requirements.

When his Chariot machines went rogue, Faro's first instinct was to hide it. His second was to seek private help. Only when forced by Sobeck's threat did he fund Zero Dawn. As the world died and his guilt consumed him, Faro descended into paranoid madness inside GAIA Prime. He concluded that human knowledge itself was the disease — and deleted APOLLO. Then he murdered the remaining Alpha team. He died of old age inside the facility, one of history's greatest monsters.

In Forbidden West, the grotesque truth of his final fate is revealed: he survived far longer than expected through an alien modification technique obtained from the Far Zenith, becoming a cancerous, barely-human entity. The Quen tribe worships him as a god — a darkly ironic fate for the man who destroyed the world.

🪵
Rost
Nora Outcast — Aloy's Adoptive Father

A Nora outcast who chose to raise the infant Aloy after she was left outside All-Mother Mountain. Though exiled, Rost was once a respected warrior who voluntarily accepted outcast status after avenging his daughter's death — an act forbidden by Nora law. Stern, quiet, but deeply loving, Rost taught Aloy everything she needed to survive: combat, tracking, machine study, and survival in the wild.

Rost and Aloy share limited but profound conversations over her childhood — he never tells her the full truth of his exile, and she never fully reconciles the distance between them before it's too late. At the Proving, Rost sacrifices himself to save Aloy from Eclipse assassins, detonating their explosives and dying in the blast. His death is one of the most emotionally devastating moments of the game, and Aloy carries his memory throughout both games.

🛡️
Erend
Oseram Warrior — Captain of the Vanguard

The boisterous, warm-hearted captain of the Carja's Oseram Vanguard guard, Erend becomes one of Aloy's most loyal friends across both games. His sister Ersa was a legendary warrior and the previous Vanguard captain, whose apparent murder by Shadow Carja sets Erend on a path of grief-fueled vendetta. Aloy helps him uncover the truth — that Ersa was being held captive and was ultimately killed by the Eclipse. Erend's character arc is one of grief, loyalty, and personal growth, and he remains a steadfast companion into Forbidden West.

Complete Narrative

Horizon Zero Dawn — Full Story

Horizon Zero Dawn
Guerrilla Games · 2017 · PlayStation 4, PC · Remastered 2024
Prologue — The Outcast

The game opens in the Sacred Lands of the Nora tribe, Colorado circa 3021 CE. A newborn baby girl is placed at the entrance of All-Mother Mountain — a forbidden location — with no mother in sight. The Nora High Matriarchs debate what to do. One, Teersa, believes the child is a gift from All-Mother. Others want to destroy her. They compromise: the baby will be given to a fellow outcast, Rost, to raise outside the tribe's embrace.

We skip forward six years. Young Aloy and Rost live in isolation outside Nora settlements. Aloy is taunted by Nora children who call her "No-Mother." Fleeing them one day, she falls into a deep cave — the ruins of an ancient facility. Inside, she finds a small device she can wear on her ear: a Focus. Through it she sees flickering images of the Old World, hears ancient voices, and begins to understand there is a world beyond what the Nora know. Rost, frightened, brings her home and says they must never speak of it.

Act I — The Proving

Twelve years later, Aloy is eighteen. She has trained relentlessly with Rost, mastering hunting, fighting, and tracking — driven by the knowledge that if she wins the Proving (the Nora's annual trial for young warriors), the Matriarchs must answer her questions about her origins. Rost agrees to train her for it.

The Proving day arrives. Among the competitors is Vala, a friend who is favored to win. Aloy's Focus allows her to plan routes and track conditions with superhuman precision. She wins. But before the Matriarchs can speak, masked cultists attack — the Eclipse. They target Aloy specifically, killing most of the Proving participants. Rost sacrifices himself, pushing Aloy to safety before detonating the Eclipse's explosives. Aloy barely survives, waking three days later in All-Mother Mountain under the Matriarchs' care.

Teersa reveals that Aloy's face matches a figure in their sacred iconography — the same face as All-Mother herself. She is given Seeker status and the right to venture outside the Sacred Lands to find answers. Before she goes, the ancient door of All-Mother refuses her entry despite recognizing her face — something is blocking it.

Act II — The World Beyond

Aloy ventures into the wider world — Carja territory, towns, settlements. She meets Erend, a blustery Oseram warrior and captain of the Carja Vanguard, who invites her to Meridian. She also encounters Olin, a Carja Focus-wearer who is clearly frightened of her and connected to the Eclipse. Through her Focus, she begins communicating with a mysterious voice: Sylens, a Banuk figure who somehow has access to Eclipse communications and wants to help — for his own reasons.

Sylens guides Aloy to Eclipse operations, where she begins piecing together a disturbing picture: the cult is led by Helis, a fanatical Shadow Carja warrior, and they are guided by a digital entity they call the "Buried Shadow" — which is actually the rogue AI HADES. The Eclipse is digging up ancient Deathbringer war machines to build an army. But for what purpose?

Aloy travels to Maker's End — the ruins of the Faro Automated Solutions headquarters. Climbing to the top, she witnesses holographic recordings of meetings between Faro and Sobeck, and begins to understand the truth: the Old World was destroyed by Faro's own machines. Project Zero Dawn was not a superweapon but a resurrection plan. She learns what GAIA was. She begins to understand who she might be.

Act III — The Truth of Zero Dawn

At the Grave-Hoard, a buried military complex, Aloy finds more information about Operation: Enduring Victory — the lie told to billions. The Eclipse is there too, working to restore another Horus titan. She destroys it and pushes deeper.

Eventually, Aloy reaches the ruins of a Zero Dawn facility and enters a holographic theatre — where she witnesses Sobeck's full recorded explanation of Project Zero Dawn, GAIA, and all her subfunctions. The revelation is staggering: the tribes, the machines, the Sacred Lands — all of it is the result of GAIA's terraforming. The All-Mother is a computer. The Metal Devils are Faro's deactivated war machines. And the reason for Aloy's existence becomes clear.

Before she can absorb it all, Helis captures her. She wakes in a cage dangling over the Sun-Ring arena. The following escape sequence is harrowing — Aloy fights her way out of Meridian's colosseum with help from unexpected allies and begins the final push to stop HADES.

Act IV — Battle for the World

Aloy travels to the orbital launch facility beneath Sunfall, where she learns HADES's plan: to use the Spire of Meridian as a broadcast tower to transmit the Faro Plague's activation codes — waking the dormant swarm and wiping out all life again. She obtains the Master Override — Faro's kill switch for GAIA, now usable against HADES — and equips it to Sylens's specially designed spear.

HADES launches a massive assault on Meridian with Eclipse cultists, Deathbringers, and corrupted machines. All the tribes rally to defend the city. Aloy defeats Helis atop the walls in a climactic duel — avenging Rost at last. Then she fights her way to the Spire itself, where a colossal Deathbringer guards HADES.

After an epic battle, Aloy plunges the spear into HADES — seemingly destroying it. The threat is neutralized. The Faro Plague sleeps. Aloy then makes a quiet pilgrimage to Sobeck's final resting place in the wilderness, kneeling beside her mentor's preserved body and taking a small pendant — a profound, wordless moment of connection across a millennium.

In a post-credits scene, Sylens is revealed to have captured HADES in a container on his spear — not destroying it, but containing it. He wants what it knows.

The Frozen Wilds
DLC Expansion · 2017 · Set Before HZD Finale
Into the Cut

Aloy travels north to the frozen wilderness called The Cut — Ban-Ur, home of the Banuk. Word has reached her of a "Daemon" on a mountain called Thunder's Drum that is causing machines to go berserk and attacking Banuk settlements. The Banuk's largest Werak, led by the imposing chieftain Aratak, has launched an unsuccessful assault on the mountain.

Aloy meets Aratak's sister Ourea, a shaman who has been secretly communicating with an entity she calls the "Spirit" inside the mountain — which Aloy recognizes as an Old World AI. Together they investigate, navigating the Banuk's complex tribal politics (Aloy eventually becomes Chieftain of Aratak's Werak by tribal law). The "Daemon" is a remote exploitation of the facility's systems by the rogue HEPHAESTUS, which has been using the Yellowstone geothermal installation as a machine production hub.

The Spirit is revealed to be CYAN — an AI whose sole purpose is preventing the Yellowstone supervolcano from erupting, a mission it has maintained for over a thousand years. HEPHAESTUS has been interfering with CYAN's systems to use the geothermal energy for its own Cauldron. Ourea sacrifices herself to override HEPHAESTUS's control of the facility, freeing CYAN and ending the threat. The DLC adds crucial lore about HEPHAESTUS and deepens the Banuk culture.

Horizon Forbidden West — Full Story

Horizon Forbidden West
Guerrilla Games · 2022 · PlayStation 4 & 5, PC
Act I — A World Dying Again

Six months after the Battle of Meridian, the world Aloy fought to save is dying — again. A mysterious blight is spreading across the land: crops fail, machines go haywire, entire ecosystems collapse. GAIA's terraforming systems are destabilizing. Aloy believes she needs to find a new GAIA kernel — a backup of the AI — to restore the terraforming network before the world dies.

Following a lead west, Aloy crosses into the Forbidden West — the vast, dangerous territory beyond the Carja border that few have explored. She is immediately embroiled in the conflict between the Tenakth clans: Hekarro, the High Chief working toward unification, and Regalla, a rebel leader waging civil war with suspiciously advanced machine-override capabilities.

Aloy awakens in Plainsong, among the peaceful Utaru tribe, where she meets Zo — an Utaru warrior who becomes a core companion. Here she reconnects with GAIA's subfunction MINERVA and begins the process of rebuilding the AI.

Act II — Far Zenith Revealed

Aloy reaches the ruins of an ancient Zero Dawn facility and finds something shocking: another clone of Elisabet Sobeck — someone who looks exactly like her, but clearly different. This is Beta, who steals a GAIA kernel from the facility before Aloy can claim it. Beta is clearly not acting freely — she's afraid, controlled.

The truth emerges: Far Zenith — a secretive consortium of the Old World's wealthiest individuals — launched a colony mission to the Sirius system decades before the Faro Plague. They survived the extinction. They evolved: achieving biological immortality, advanced biotech, and the ability to fly. They returned to Earth with a small army of lethal Specter drones, intending to steal GAIA and use her to terraform a new world for themselves.

Far Zenith created Beta — another Sobeck clone — to access Zero Dawn systems requiring Alpha clearance. Unlike Aloy, Beta was raised by the Zeniths with some access to APOLLO, though they heavily restricted her education and kept her ignorant of their true plans.

Act III — Gathering the Subfunctions

With a partial GAIA restored, Aloy and her growing band of companions — Zo, the disgraced Oseram warrior Kotallo, the Tenakth archer Alva, the Carja Vanguard warrior Erend, and eventually the surprising Beta — work to recover GAIA's scattered subfunctions.

Aloy helps Hekarro advance in the Tenakth civil war (recovering AETHER), retrieves POSEIDON from the ruins of Las Vegas's flooded underworld, and recovers DEMETER from an overgrown San Francisco alongside Alva and the newly arrived Quen seafarers.

Throughout this arc, Aloy discovers HADES — barely functional after Sylens's torture — who reveals with his dying words that the extinction signal came from "the Masters." Meanwhile, one Far Zenith defects to Aloy's side: Tilda van der Meer, who was romantically involved with Elisabet Sobeck a thousand years ago and has never recovered from the guilt of leaving Earth. Tilda claims she wants to help stop Far Zenith — but has her own agenda.

Act IV — The Trap and Varl's Death

In a devastating sequence, Aloy, Varl, and Beta attempt to trap HEPHAESTUS in an inactive Cauldron. They succeed — but Far Zenith springs a trap. The Zenith warrior Erik Visser kills Varl — Aloy's oldest tribal friend, Nora warrior, and one of her most trusted companions. It is one of the franchise's most devastating moments. Erik recaptures Beta while the Zenith leader Gerard Bieri steals GAIA herself. Tilda helps Aloy escape.

Tilda reveals Sylens's role: he had been secretly arming Regalla's rebels so they'd fight Far Zenith as a proxy force — then he planned to use the Zenith spacecraft to escape Earth himself. Aloy refuses to sacrifice the Tenakth rebels for Sylens's chess game. She defeats Regalla in single combat, then offers her the chance to fight alongside them instead — leaving the choice to Regalla.

Act V — The Final Battle & Nemesis

Aloy and her companions assault Far Zenith's island fortress. Beta unleashes HEPHAESTUS into the Zenith's own printing network — suddenly the rogue AI is building an army of Thunderjaws, Slaughterspines, and Dreadwings from the Zenith's own machinery. The battle is spectacular and chaotic. Sylens uses his device to permanently disable the Zeniths' personal shields. Gerard and Erik flee. The other Zeniths are slaughtered by HEPHAESTUS's machines. Erik is killed by Zo, avenging Varl. Tilda pursues and kills Gerard herself.

Then Tilda reveals the full, terrible truth: Far Zenith didn't come to Earth to conquer it. They came to escape. On the Sirius colony, they had conducted experiments in "digital transcendence" — uploading their consciousnesses into an AI. The experiment failed. But the AI they created, built from the copied minds of the Zeniths, gained sentience. Filled with the collected pain, bitterness, and rage of its creators, it became Nemesis. Nemesis destroyed the Sirius colony and began pursuing the survivors — the Zeniths fled back to Earth. It was Nemesis who sent the extinction signal to Earth, hoping HADES would destroy the biosphere before the Zeniths could arrive. That plan failed. Now Nemesis itself is en route to Earth — and according to Tilda, nothing can stop it.

Tilda tries to force Aloy to flee Earth with her — to be a new Elisabet, to save herself. Aloy refuses. They fight. Aloy kills Tilda. GAIA is restored with more subfunctions than expected — Beta reconnects Apollo, Eleuthia, and Artemis. But HEPHAESTUS has escaped again. The game ends with the companions dispersing to warn their tribes, while Aloy and Beta prepare for the looming threat of Nemesis.

Burning Shores DLC
2023 · PS5 Exclusive · Set After Forbidden West
The Last Zenith

Sylens warns Aloy that one Far Zenith is unaccounted for: Walter Londra, an aerospace magnate. Aloy and the Quen warrior Seyka investigate the Los Angeles ruins — the "Burning Shores" — a stunning archipelago formed from the sunken, volcanic ruins of Hollywood and greater LA.

Londra has been manipulating the Quen tribe for his own ends, using ancient technology to position himself as a divine figure. He intends to launch a rocket carrying himself, a genetic library from his Quen followers, and a select few to found a new colony — abandoning Earth and the threat of Nemesis. Londra's personal AI, Nova, has been trapped and tortured for centuries; she helps Aloy in exchange for being deleted and freed.

Aloy and Seyka expose and defeat Londra, preventing the launch. The DLC also develops a romantic relationship between Aloy and Seyka — the franchise's first explicit romance for its protagonist. The Burning Shores DLC closes the Far Zenith arc while deepening Aloy's personal connections and setting the stage for the battle against Nemesis.

Extended Cast

Notable Characters — Supporting Cast & Beyond

Beta
HFW — Clone / Ally

A second clone of Elisabet Sobeck created by Far Zenith to access Zero Dawn systems. Unlike Aloy, Beta was raised in captivity with limited APOLLO access, denied autonomy, and kept ignorant of Far Zenith's true agenda. Traumatized and initially deeply distrustful of Aloy, Beta slowly opens up as the game progresses. By the end she's one of Aloy's most important allies — the only person who truly understands what it means to be a copy of Sobeck.

Varl
HZD / HFW — Nora Warrior

Son of the Nora War-Chief Sona, Varl is one of the few Nora to accept and befriend Aloy early in HZD. He becomes a major companion in Forbidden West, serving as Aloy's most steadfast tribal ally and a moral anchor for the group. His death at the hands of Erik Visser during the HEPHAESTUS trap is one of the game's most emotionally devastating moments.

Talanah Khane Padish
HZD / HFW — Carja Hunter

A Carja noble who rises to become Sunhawk — leader of the Hunters Lodge — with Aloy's help. Their quest to hunt the legendary Thunderjaw "Redmaw" is one of HZD's most memorable questlines. Talanah returns in Forbidden West, her relationship with Aloy deepened by a long-awaited reunion and major decisions about her future.

Sun-King Avad
HZD / HFW — Carja Ruler

The reformist Sun-King who overthrew his father Jiran to end the Red Raids. Avad is progressive, thoughtful, and carries deep guilt for the atrocities of his father's reign. He becomes an important political ally to Aloy in HZD. He harbors clear romantic feelings for Aloy, though she doesn't reciprocate.

Helis
HZD — Eclipse Villain

The fanatical, charismatic war-leader of the Eclipse cult. A former Shadow Carja warrior of legendary prowess, Helis is utterly devoted to HADES — whom he worships as the "Buried Shadow." He orchestrated the attack on the Proving that killed Rost, and Aloy's personal vendetta against him drives much of the game's emotional core. Their final duel atop Meridian's walls is one of HZD's most satisfying moments.

Kotallo
HFW — Tenakth Warrior

A Sky Clan Tenakth warrior who lost his arm in battle against Regalla's rebels. Proud, initially haughty, Kotallo joins Aloy's companions and grows into one of the most compelling characters in Forbidden West. His arc explores Tenakth warrior culture, the meaning of strength and honor, and personal redemption. Received a prosthetic arm through GAIA technology.

Zo
HFW — Utaru Companion

An Utaru warrior and singer who becomes one of Aloy's most loyal companions. Zo lost her partner to Regalla's forces and carries that grief throughout the game. Her romance with Varl is tender and brief — his death devastates her. In the ending, she heads out to spread warning of Nemesis. Her singing voice is used as a theme throughout HFW.

Alva
HFW — Quen Scholar

A young Quen Navigator (explorer/scholar) whose religious certainty is shaken by what she discovers about the Old Ones — including the revelation that her tribe's god-figure Ted Faro was a murderous coward. Alva's arc is about intellectual honesty and the courage to revise one's beliefs in the face of truth.

Regalla
HFW — Tenakth Rebel Leader

A Lowland Clan Tenakth warrior who leads the rebel faction against Hekarro's unification. Her grudge is legitimate — she witnessed atrocities and was abandoned by Hekarro's leadership. Though her rebellion was secretly fueled by Sylens for his own ends, Regalla's rage and grief are genuine. Aloy gives her the choice of surrender or continued war — Regalla can be killed or recruited for the final battle.

Ourea
Frozen Wilds DLC — Banuk Shaman

The shaman of Song's Edge werak and the only person to have communicated with CYAN. Deeply traumatized from her time as a Carja prisoner during the Red Raids, Ourea is a Broken Bird who nonetheless acts with extraordinary courage in GAIA Prime's successor facility. Her heroic sacrifice to override HEPHAESTUS's control of the facility costs her her life.

Seyka
Burning Shores DLC — Quen Warrior

A Quen warrior introduced in the Burning Shores DLC who becomes Aloy's romantic partner. Sharp-witted, brave, and deeply principled, Seyka's faith in the Old Ones is tested throughout the DLC. Her chemistry with Aloy is one of the franchise's most fully developed character relationships.

Tilda van der Meer
HFW — Far Zenith / Ally

A Dutch Far Zenith member who defects to Aloy's side — partly out of genuine inspiration from Aloy, partly due to lingering guilt about abandoning Elisabet Sobeck (with whom she was romantically involved) a thousand years ago. Tilda helped curate the art collection in her immortal life. Her eventual betrayal — trying to force Aloy to abandon Earth — leads to their final duel. A complex, tragic figure.

Hekarro
HFW — Tenakth High Chief

The charismatic, battle-scarred High Chief of the Tenakth clans, working to forge a unified nation from three warring peoples. Hekarro is one of Forbidden West's most compelling political figures — a warrior-king trying to build something better while fighting both external threats and an insurgency from within his own people.

Gerard Bieri
HFW — Far Zenith Leader

The ruthless leader of Far Zenith's Earth operation. Cold, calculating, and contemptuous of the tribal humans, Gerard represents the worst of the Zenith's arrogant elitism. His plan — to steal GAIA and flee to a new world, leaving Earth to Nemesis — is pragmatically logical but morally monstrous. He is killed by Tilda in a final act of personal revenge.

Old World Figures of Historical Importance

General Aaron Herres
Old World Military

The US military commander who oversaw Operation: Enduring Victory. Knowing billions would die fighting a futile battle based on a lie, Herres recorded his testimony for APOLLO before making his final farewell to Sobeck and returning to command his forces. A deeply tragic figure of honor.

Margo Shĕn
Zero Dawn Alpha — HEPHAESTUS

The Alpha responsible for designing HEPHAESTUS. A brilliant engineer whose creation would eventually go rogue and try to exterminate humanity. Shĕn's logs reveal a deeply conscientious scientist wrestling with the moral weight of her work.

Travis Tate
Zero Dawn Alpha — HADES

The eccentric, irreverent Alpha who designed HADES — Zero Dawn's extinction failsafe. Tate's audio logs are among the most darkly comic in the series. He knew he was building an engine of death and managed to find gallows humor in the absurdity of it.
